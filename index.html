Definition

Hiatus hernias occur when there is herniation of abdominal contents through the esophageal hiatus of the diaphragm into the thoracic cavity
Epidemiology

The prevalence of hiatus hernia increases with age, estimates go from about 10% under age 40 to ~30-50% in people over 50–70 years, with a slight female predilection.
Clinical presentation

Many patients with a hiatus hernia are asymptomatic, and it is an incidental finding. However, symptoms may include epigastric or chest pain, postprandial fullness, nausea and vomiting. Sometimes hiatus hernias are considered synonymous with GERD, but there is a poor correlation between the two conditions.
Type

Type 1: sliding hiatal hernia (~95%)
Type 2: paraesophageal hiatal hernia with the gastro-esophageal junction in a normal position
Type 3: mixed or compound type
Type 4: with additional herniation of viscera
CT features

Herniated organs above the diaphragm
Widened esophageal hiatus >15mm
Treatment and prognosis

Symptomatic hiatus hernias should be managed surgically
Asymptomatic → conservative
Complications

Gastric volvulus, obstruction, ischemia, tension gastrothorax
Differential diagnosis

Retrocardiac lung abscess
Retrocardiac empyema
Epiphrenic esophageal diverticulum
